\chapter{Methodology}\label{chap:methods}

\section{Overview}
This chapter details the methodological pipeline designed to develop and validate predictive models for SA--AKI mortality. The workflow begins with the construction of a high-fidelity cohort from an ontology-integrated data warehouse. It proceeds through three analytical stages: (i) exploratory data analysis for phenotyping, (ii) development of a supervised binary classifier for mortality (the primary task), and (iii) time-to-event survival analysis. A strict, one-time stratified 80/20 data split underpins the entire process to prevent information leakage. All data-dependent transformations are fitted on the training set only and then applied to the test set for a single final evaluation. The development process is guided by the TRIPOD statement for transparent reporting.

\section{Data Sources, ETL, and Cohort (T0--T24)}
\textbf{ETL and Integration.} The analysis asset was built by ingesting and integrating multiple sources, primarily MIMIC--IV, with medical ontologies (RxNorm, ATC, SNOMED~CT) linked via the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS). This was managed in a PostgreSQL database, enabling reproducible, ingredient-level value sets for concepts like systemic antibiotics and vasopressors. A validation suite ensured unit harmonization (e.g., F$\rightarrow$C, lb$\rightarrow$kg), referential integrity, and logical consistency.

\textbf{MIMIC--IV Dataset.} The Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care IV (MIMIC--IV v3.1) is a large, freely accessible electronic health record dataset comprising de-identified data from patients admitted to the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (BIDMC) in Boston, Massachusetts, between 2008 and 2022 \citep{Johnson_2023_MIMIC}. The database contains over 430,000 hospital admissions and approximately 73,000 ICU stays. For this study, the following MIMIC--IV modules were used:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Core module (\texttt{hosp}):} Patient demographics, admission/discharge records, diagnoses (ICD--9/10--CM), procedures, and pharmacy data. This module provided the foundation for patient identification, comorbidity extraction (Charlson index components), and outcome ascertainment (in-hospital death).
    \item \textbf{ICU module (\texttt{icu}):} High-resolution time-stamped charted events including vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, SpO$_2$), fluid inputs/outputs, and ventilator settings. This module was the primary source for T0--T24 vital sign aggregates, fluid balance calculations, and urine output measurements.
    \item \textbf{Laboratory module (within \texttt{hosp}):} Time-stamped laboratory results including complete blood count, metabolic panel, liver function tests, coagulation studies, arterial blood gases, and inflammatory markers. Each result was mapped to standardized units before aggregation.
    \item \textbf{Microbiology module (within \texttt{hosp}):} Culture orders and results, used in conjunction with pharmacy records to identify suspected infection events for Sepsis--3 labeling.
    \item \textbf{Pharmacy/eMAR module:} Medication administration records, cross-referenced with RxNorm and ATC ontologies to identify systemic antibiotics and vasopressors at the ingredient level.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Feature Categories for Risk Framing.} The 162 features used to characterize the degree of risk within the first 24 hours were drawn from six clinically distinct domains, each contributing a different dimension of the patient's early physiological state:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Demographics and baseline (4 features):} Age, sex, admission weight, and AKI trigger type. These establish the patient's baseline characteristics.
    \item \textbf{Comorbidity burden (18 features):} Charlson comorbidity index components and total score, capturing the chronic disease context (e.g., diabetes, liver disease, metastatic cancer) that modulates acute risk.
    \item \textbf{Severity scores (8 features):} APACHE~III score and SOFA sub-scores (respiratory, cardiovascular, hepatic, coagulation, renal, neurological) plus the total, providing validated composite measures of organ dysfunction severity.
    \item \textbf{Vital signs and hemodynamics ($\sim$50 features):} Heart rate, blood pressures (systolic, diastolic, MAP), respiratory rate, SpO$_2$, and shock index, each summarized by multiple temporal statistics (first, last, median, IQR, range, delta, slope, AUC, count) to capture both the level and trajectory of physiological derangement.
    \item \textbf{Laboratory and blood gas panel ($\sim$70 features):} Renal function (creatinine, BUN), metabolic state (glucose, bicarbonate, anion gap, lactate), hepatic function (AST, ALT, bilirubin, albumin, ALP), hematology (hemoglobin, platelets, WBC, RDW, INR), electrolytes (sodium, potassium, chloride, calcium, magnesium, phosphate), oxygenation (PaO$_2$, PaCO$_2$, pH, PF ratio), and inflammatory markers (LDH, troponin). Each analyte is summarized by count, median, min, max, and where applicable, delta and QC fractions.
    \item \textbf{Fluid balance and therapies ($\sim$12 features):} Net fluid balance, urine output (absolute and weight-indexed), and binary flags for mechanical ventilation, vasopressor use, and RRT within 24 hours.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent This multi-domain feature set ensures that the model has access to a comprehensive physiological snapshot at the decision point, spanning organ-specific dysfunction markers, systemic severity, treatment intensity, and temporal dynamics.

\textbf{Cohort Definition.} The study population was defined as adults ($\geq$18\,y) on their first ICU stay with a length-of-stay $\geq$24\,h. The SA--AKI cohort was identified using established clinical criteria:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sepsis (Sepsis--3):} Defined by suspected infection (a body-fluid culture near the administration of a systemic antibiotic) and an acute increase in organ dysfunction ($\Delta\mathrm{SOFA}\geq 2$). The sepsis onset time, $t_{\text{sepsis}}$, was the start of the first qualifying ICU day.
    \item \textbf{AKI (KDIGO 2012):} Defined by changes in serum creatinine (SCr) or urine output. A hierarchical method established baseline SCr. The AKI onset time, $t_{\text{aki}}$, was the earliest time any criterion was met.
    \item \textbf{SA--AKI:} Required AKI onset within the window $[t_{\text{sepsis}}-48\text{ h},\,t_{\text{sepsis}}+48\text{ h}]$.
\end{itemize}
Patients with prior End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) or who received Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT) within the first 24\,h were excluded. All predictive features were restricted to measurements within the first 24 hours of ICU admission (T0--T24) to ensure clinical actionability and prevent look-ahead bias.

\section{Data Split and Leakage Control}
A one-time, stratified 80/20 train-test split was performed on the cohort. Let $\mathcal{D}=\{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ be the full dataset, where $x_i\in\mathbb{R}^p$ are the T0--T24 features and $y_i\in\{0,1\}$ is the in-hospital death indicator. The partition is
\[
\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}\;\dot\cup\;\mathcal{D}_{\text{test}},
\]
where partitions are disjoint and stratified by the outcome to maintain class balance. All data-dependent objects—imputation models, scaling parameters, and encoding vocabularies—were fitted \emph{only} on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ and then applied as frozen transformers to $\mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}$. Any validation set used for model tuning or calibration was created as a further split \emph{from within} $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$.

\textbf{Leakage Audit.} A scripted audit ensured that no variables encoding information after T24 were included as predictors. This included removing features like total ICU length of stay and aggregates (e.g., `\_n` observation counts) that could act as proxies for care intensity or illness duration.

\subsection{What Is Data Leakage and Why It Matters}
Data leakage occurs when information that would not be available at the time of prediction is inadvertently used during model training or evaluation, leading to artificially inflated performance metrics that do not reflect real-world utility. In the context of SA--AKI mortality prediction, leakage can manifest through several distinct channels:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Temporal leakage (look-ahead bias):} When features are derived from measurements taken \emph{after} the intended prediction time. For example, if a model is meant to predict mortality at 24 hours post-admission but uses laboratory values or vital signs recorded on day 3 or later, it has access to information about the patient's clinical trajectory that a clinician would not have at the decision point. Whole-stay aggregation of time-varying biomarkers (e.g., computing the mean creatinine from admission to discharge) is a common source of this leakage. Similarly, including ICU length of stay as a predictor directly encodes outcome-correlated information: patients who die early have short stays, and survivors have longer ones.

    \item \textbf{Preprocessing leakage (train--test contamination):} When data-dependent transformations—such as imputation, feature scaling, or feature selection—are fitted on the entire dataset (including the test set) before splitting. This allows statistical properties of the test data (means, variances, correlations with the outcome) to influence the training pipeline. For instance, if median imputation is computed across all patients and then the data is split, the imputed values in the training set carry information from test-set patients. The same applies to feature selection: if a Lasso or correlation filter is applied globally, the selected features reflect associations present in the evaluation data.

    \item \textbf{Target leakage:} When a predictor is a direct proxy for the outcome variable. Including length of stay in a mortality model is a canonical example: it is not a cause or early indicator of death but rather a consequence of the same process that determines the outcome.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The practical consequence of leakage is that a model appears to perform well during development but fails when deployed prospectively, because the leaked information is no longer available. This is why several published SA--AKI models report AUROC values of 0.85--0.88 in internal testing but have not demonstrated comparable performance in external or prospective settings. Our pipeline was designed from the outset to eliminate all three channels: the T0--T24 window prevents temporal leakage, the train-only fitting of all transformers prevents preprocessing leakage, and the scripted audit removes target proxies.

\subsection{The 24-Hour Window: Rationale and Limitations}
\label{sec:24h-rationale}
The choice of a 24-hour observation window after ICU admission (T0--T24) is motivated by both clinical workflow and methodological discipline:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Clinical actionability:} In most ICU workflows, the first 24 hours represent the period during which initial stabilization, diagnostic workup, and early therapeutic decisions are made. A risk score available at the end of this window can inform decisions about escalation of care, nephrology consultation, or goals-of-care discussions at a time when intervention can still alter the trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Data completeness:} The first 24 hours typically yield the richest set of measurements—admission labs, initial vital sign trends, early fluid balance, and severity scores (APACHE~III, SOFA)—providing a comprehensive snapshot of the patient's physiological state.
    \item \textbf{Temporal discipline:} A fixed, prospectively defined window eliminates the ambiguity of variable-length observation periods and prevents the inadvertent inclusion of post-decision information.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Heterogeneity of arrival acuity.} A legitimate concern is that patients arrive at the ICU at different stages of their illness. Some patients are admitted directly from the emergency department with early sepsis, while others are transferred from general wards after days of deterioration, and still others arrive post-operatively. This heterogeneity means that the ``first 24 hours of ICU admission'' captures different phases of the disease process for different patients. We address this in several ways:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Severity scores as normalizers:} The APACHE~III score, computed at admission, and the day-1 SOFA score both serve as summary measures of illness severity at the time of ICU entry. By including these as features, the model implicitly adjusts for differences in baseline acuity. A patient who arrives late in their septic course will typically have higher SOFA and APACHE~III scores, and the model learns to weight these accordingly.
    \item \textbf{Comorbidity encoding:} The Charlson comorbidity index components capture the chronic disease burden that shapes the patient's trajectory prior to ICU admission, providing context for the acute measurements.
    \item \textbf{Cohort definition anchoring:} The SA--AKI cohort definition requires that both sepsis onset and AKI onset occur within a defined temporal relationship ($\pm$48\,h). This ensures that the cohort is clinically homogeneous in terms of the acute syndrome, even if the timing relative to hospital admission varies.
    \item \textbf{Acknowledged limitation:} Despite these mitigations, the heterogeneity of arrival timing remains a source of irreducible noise. Patients admitted very early in their septic course may have less informative 24-hour data than those admitted later. This is a fundamental limitation of any fixed-window approach and is explicitly acknowledged as a factor that may attenuate model discrimination. Future work exploring variable-length windows or time-series models could address this limitation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Preprocessing and Feature Engineering (Train-Only)}
\textbf{Missingness Analysis.} The nature of missing data was formally investigated on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$. Little's MCAR test was performed, and its rejection of the null hypothesis suggested that data were not Missing Completely at Random (MCAR). This justified a more careful handling of missingness beyond simple imputation.

\textbf{Missingness Indicators.} For every feature with missing values, a binary indicator variable (\texttt{\_missing}) was created. The association between each indicator and the mortality outcome was tested using a chi-square test. Only indicators that showed a statistically significant association ($p<0.05$) were retained and appended to the dataset as new features, thereby preserving potentially prognostic information contained in the pattern of missingness.

\textbf{Imputation.} After adding significant missingness indicators, remaining missing values in numerical columns were imputed using the median calculated from the training set.

\textbf{Feature Selection and Scaling.} Highly correlated features (Pearson correlation > 0.99) were identified, and one from each pair was removed to reduce multicollinearity. All numerical features were then standardized using `StandardScaler`, with the scaler being fit only on the training data.

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis and Phenotyping (Training Only)}
To understand the cohort's clinical characteristics, an extensive EDA was performed on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$. This served as an unsupervised phenotyping step. Key analyses included:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Distributional Analysis:} Visualizing the distributions of key continuous variables (e.g., age, APACHE III, SOFA score, creatinine, lactate) and comparing them between survivor and non-survivor groups using histograms and density plots.
    \item \textbf{Correlation Analysis:} Calculating the Pearson correlation of all features with the mortality outcome to identify the strongest univariate predictors.
    \item \textbf{Stratified Analysis:} Using violin plots, box plots, and heatmaps to compare vital signs, laboratory values, and SOFA sub-scores between outcomes. Statistical significance was assessed using the Mann-Whitney U test due to the non-normal distribution of most variables.
\end{itemize}

\section{Supervised Binary Mortality Prediction (Primary)}
\textbf{Learning Objective.} For a model $f_\theta:\mathbb{R}^p\!\to\![0,1]$, the goal was to minimize the average binary cross-entropy loss on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ using 5-fold cross-validation.

\textbf{Model Benchmarking.} A comprehensive set of models was benchmarked: Logistic Regression, LDA, QDA, Naive Bayes, regularized linear models, Decision Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost, and LightGBM. For tree-based ensembles, hyperparameters were tuned using Optuna. The impact of class imbalance was addressed primarily through `class\_weight='balanced'` and was compared against various oversampling strategies (e.g., SMOTE, ADASYN) in sensitivity analyses.

\textbf{Final Model Selection, Calibration, and Thresholding.} The LightGBM model was selected based on its superior performance. The final pipeline involved:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Training the optimized LightGBM model on a sub-split of the training data.
    \item Calibrating the model's outputs on a held-out validation split (from the training set) using `CalibratedClassifierCV` with the isotonic method.
    \item Determining the optimal classification threshold on the same validation split by maximizing Youden's J statistic.
    \item Retraining the final, calibrated LightGBM model on the entire training dataset ($\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$).
    \item Evaluating the final model on the untouched test set ($\mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}$) using the predetermined threshold.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Interpretability.} The final LightGBM model was interpreted using SHAP. A beeswarm summary plot was generated to visualize global feature importance and the direction of effects.

\section{Survival Analysis (Time to Death)}
The same preprocessed T0--T24 feature matrix was used to model time to in-hospital death, with patients discharged alive being right-censored.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cox Proportional Hazards (PH):} A CoxPH model was fitted using the `lifelines` library. Feature selection was performed by retaining the top 50 predictors from an initial full model, and the penalizer was tuned. Results were interpreted via a forest plot of hazard ratios.
    \item \textbf{DeepSurv:} A custom neural network-based Cox model was implemented in PyTorch, using SELU activations and residual connections. The model was trained by minimizing the negative Cox partial log-likelihood with the Efron method for handling ties.
\end{itemize}
Model performance was assessed by Harrell's C-index, time-dependent AUC, calibration plots for 7, 14, and 28-day horizons, and Kaplan-Meier curves stratified by model-predicted risk quartiles. The statistical significance of the risk stratification was confirmed with the log-rank test.

\section{Adherence to TRIPOD Guidelines}
This study was developed in accordance with the TRIPOD (Transparent Reporting of a multivariable prediction model for Individual Prognosis Or Diagnosis) guidelines. Key items addressed include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Source of Data (Item 4a):} The study design and source of data (MIMIC-IV, a large electronic health record database) are clearly described.
    \item \textbf{Participants (Item 5a-b):} The study setting (ICU), location, and detailed eligibility criteria for participants are specified in the cohort construction.
    \item \textbf{Outcome and Predictors (Items 6a, 7a):} The outcome (in-hospital mortality) and all predictors (derived from the T0--T24 window) are explicitly defined.
    \item \textbf{Sample Size (Item 8):} The flow of participants through the study is described, detailing exclusions and the final sample size available for analysis.
    \item \textbf{Missing Data (Item 9):} The handling of missing data is detailed, including statistical testing of the missingness mechanism, creation and selection of significant missingness indicators, and median imputation based on the training set.
    \item \textbf{Statistical Analysis Methods (Item 10a-d):} Predictor handling (correlation removal, scaling), model-building procedures (benchmarking of multiple algorithms, Optuna for tuning), methods for internal validation (stratified 80/20 train-test split), and all performance measures (AUROC, F1-score, C-index, calibration plots, DCA) are specified.
    \item \textbf{Model Specification and Performance (Items 15a, 16):} The final model (LightGBM) and its key hyperparameters are presented. Performance measures on the test set are reported, providing an unbiased estimate of the model's performance on new data.
\end{itemize}
